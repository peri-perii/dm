Module 01 · Leadership

What Is Leadership?

Understanding the distinction between leadership and management, and the roles each plays in organisational success.

Core Definitions

Leadership
Influence Toward Goals

The ability to influence a group toward the achievement of a shared vision or set of goals.

Management
Authority & Compliance

Use of authority inherent in a designated formal rank to obtain compliance from organisational members.

Roles of Leaders vs Managers
Establish
Organisational Vision
→
Formulate Strategy
for Vision
→
Implement
Strategy
← Leader's Role Manager's Role →

While leadership focuses on setting direction and inspiring followers, management concentrates on executing processes and maintaining order. Effective organisations need both — visionary leaders AND competent managers.

Leadership Theory Overview

Leadership research has evolved through four major paradigms:

Era 1
Trait Theories

Leaders are born with innate qualities that differentiate them.

Era 2
Behavioural Theories

Leadership behaviours can be learned and taught.

Era 3
Contingency Theories

Effective leadership depends on situational context.

Era 4
Contemporary Issues

Transformational, servant, charismatic leadership styles.

Case Study

Satya Nadella at Microsoft

When Satya Nadella became CEO of Microsoft in 2014, the company was widely regarded as stagnating — a tech giant losing relevance. As a leader (not just a manager), Nadella articulated a new vision: "Mobile-first, cloud-first world." He shifted the culture from a "know-it-all" to a "learn-it-all" mindset.

His management team then implemented this vision operationally — restructuring divisions, pivoting Azure, and acquiring GitHub. The result: Microsoft's market capitalisation grew from ~$300 billion to over $2 trillion under his tenure.

Key Insight

Nadella demonstrated that leadership (vision + culture shift) and management (execution + resource allocation) must work in tandem. Neither alone would have achieved the turnaround.

Practice Questions

Q1. What is the fundamental difference between leadership and management?
+

Leadership involves influencing others toward a goal — it is about inspiration, vision, and direction. Management involves using formal authority to obtain compliance — it is about planning, organising, and controlling resources.

A key distinction: you can be a manager without being a leader (authority without influence) or a leader without being a manager (influence without formal power).

Q2. Can a person be both a leader and a manager simultaneously? Justify.
+

Yes. The most effective organisational figures combine both. They use their positional authority (management) while simultaneously inspiring and influencing their teams (leadership). Research suggests that organisations with leader-managers outperform those with only one or the other.

Q3. Why did early leadership theories focus on traits rather than behaviours?
+

Early researchers assumed leadership was an innate quality — that great leaders were "born, not made." By studying historical leaders, they tried to identify common personality attributes. This was largely a reflection of the scientific methods of the time, which focused on selection (finding the right person) rather than development (training people to lead).

Module 02 · Leadership

Trait Theories of Leadership

Examining the personality, social, and intellectual characteristics that differentiate leaders from non-leaders.

Core Premise

Trait theories propose that certain innate characteristics make individuals natural leaders. Researchers sought to identify a universal set of traits by studying historically successful leaders.

Key Assumption

"Leaders are born, not made." — The goal is to select leaders based on traits, not develop them through training.

Core Leadership Traits

Ambition & Energy

Drive to achieve and the stamina to persist through challenges.

Desire to Lead

Genuine motivation to take charge and direct others.

Honesty & Integrity

Consistent trustworthiness that builds follower confidence.

Self-Confidence

Belief in one's own judgement and abilities.

Intelligence

Ability to gather, analyse and interpret complex information.

High Self-Monitoring

Ability to adapt one's behaviour to different social contexts.

Job-Relevant Knowledge

Deep domain expertise that enables sound decision-making.

Additionally, the Big Five personality traits (particularly Extraversion, Conscientiousness, and Openness) show consistent correlations with leadership emergence.

Limitations of Trait Theory

Critical Limitation

No universal traits have been found that predict leadership success across all situations. Traits that make someone an effective CEO may be irrelevant — or even harmful — for a frontline team leader.

  • Traits do not generalise across contexts
  • Unclear causal relationship — do traits create leaders, or does leading develop traits?
  • Better at predicting leader emergence (who gets promoted) than leader effectiveness (who achieves results)
  • Ignores the influence of followers and the environment
Case Study

Indra Nooyi — PepsiCo CEO (2006–2018)

Indra Nooyi exemplifies several core leadership traits. Her exceptional intelligence was evident from her academic record; her ambition drove PepsiCo's revenue from $35B to $63.5B. Her integrity — she regularly wrote personal letters to parents of her top executives — built deep loyalty. Her "Performance with Purpose" vision demonstrated high job-relevant knowledge and strategic foresight.

However, the Trait limitation is also visible: Nooyi's intensely strategic style worked at the executive level but reportedly created distance from operational teams, illustrating how traits effective in one context may be less so in another.

Key Insight

Nooyi possessed most classical leadership traits, but her effectiveness varied by organisational context — supporting the critique that traits alone do not guarantee situational leadership success.

Practice Questions

Q1. List and explain any four key traits associated with effective leadership.
+

1. Ambition and Energy: Effective leaders show a strong drive to achieve goals and maintain the stamina to persist.

2. Honesty and Integrity: They consistently align their words and actions, creating trust among followers.

3. Self-Confidence: They trust their judgment, which helps followers feel secure in uncertain situations.

4. Intelligence: They quickly process information to make sound decisions and adapt strategies.

Q2. Why is trait theory considered an incomplete explanation of leadership?
+

Trait theory is incomplete because: (a) no single set of traits works universally, (b) it ignores situational factors, (c) it predicts who becomes a leader, not who succeeds as one, and (d) it cannot explain why some trait-rich individuals fail while others without those traits succeed in certain contexts.

Q3. Is the assumption "leaders are born" valid in modern organisations?
+

No, this is largely outdated. Modern research and practice show leadership can be developed. Traits may provide a foundation, but behaviours, skills, emotional intelligence and adaptability can be taught. Most leadership development programmes explicitly reject the "born leader" premise, investing heavily in coaching and mentoring instead.

Module 03 · Leadership

Behavioural Theories of Leadership

Leadership can be taught — effective leaders are distinguished by what they do, not who they are.

Core Premise

Key Shift

From "Who are leaders?" (traits) to "What do leaders do?" (behaviours). This opened the door to leadership development through training.

Two landmark research programmes — the Ohio State Studies and the University of Michigan Studies — independently identified two fundamental dimensions of leader behaviour that remain influential today.

Ohio State Studies — Two Dimensions

Dimension 1
Initiating Structure

Extent to which a leader defines their role and subordinates' roles in pursuit of goal attainment. Task-focused behaviour: assigning tasks, setting deadlines, specifying procedures.

Dimension 2
Consideration

Extent to which a leader builds job relationships characterised by mutual trust, respect for ideas, and regard for feelings. People-focused behaviour.

These two dimensions are independent — a leader can be high on both, low on both, or high on one and low on the other.

University of Michigan Studies

Michigan — Type 1
Employee-Oriented Leader

Emphasises interpersonal relations; takes personal interest in employee needs; accepts individual differences. Correlates with higher group productivity and satisfaction.

Michigan — Type 2
Production-Oriented Leader

Emphasises technical or task aspects of the job. Treats employees as means to accomplish work goals.

Limitation of Behavioural Approach

Behavioural theories assume there is one best style, but research has not confirmed this universally. Effective behaviours in a startup may differ from those in a military context. This gap led to contingency theories.

Case Study

Southwest Airlines — Herb Kelleher's (9,9) Leadership

Herb Kelleher, co-founder of Southwest Airlines, exemplified the 9,9 "Team Leader" style. He was famous for being intensely task-focused (Southwest consistently ranked first in on-time performance) while simultaneously being deeply people-oriented — he memorised employees' names, worked baggage loading on holidays, and wrote personal notes to staff.

Southwest's remarkable 47 consecutive years of profitability (unmatched in the airline industry) is partly attributed to this dual-dimension leadership, which drove both operational excellence and exceptional employee loyalty.

Key Insight

High task AND high people focus is not contradictory. Kelleher demonstrated that caring deeply about employees and demanding high operational performance reinforce each other.

Practice Questions

Q1. Compare Initiating Structure and Consideration as leadership behaviours.
+

Initiating Structure is task-oriented — the leader defines roles, sets goals, organises work, and establishes deadlines. Consideration is relationship-oriented — the leader focuses on employee wellbeing, mutual respect, and trust. Ohio State research found these are independent dimensions, not opposites on a spectrum.

Q2. What does the Managerial Grid suggest about the "ideal" leadership style?
+

Blake and Mouton's grid suggests the Team Leader style (9,9) — high concern for both people and production — is the ideal. They argued this style leads to the best outcomes: motivated employees who are also productive. However, contingency theorists later challenged this by showing context matters significantly.

Q3. How does behavioural theory differ from trait theory in its practical application?
+

Trait theory focuses on selecting the right person (identify traits → hire/promote). Behavioural theory focuses on training existing people (identify behaviours → teach them). This shift has massive practical implications: instead of assuming leadership is fixed, organisations can invest in leadership development programmes.

Contingency Theories

Effective leadership depends on the situation. There is no single best leadership style — it depends on context, followers, and environment.

Key Models Overview

ModelKey IdeaLeader Style
Fiedler ModelMatch leader to situation; style is fixedTask vs Relationship-oriented
Cognitive Resource TheoryStress determines whether intelligence or experience matters moreContext-dependent
Hersey & Blanchard SLTAdapt style to follower readinessDirective / Supportive / Participative / Monitoring
LMX TheoryLeaders form different quality relationships with followersIn-group vs Out-group
Path-Goal TheoryLeader clears obstacles and rewards goal attainmentDirective / Supportive / Participative / Achievement-oriented

3.1 Fiedler Contingency Model

Fiedler's key assumption: a leader's style is fixed. Therefore, the solution is to either match leaders to situations or change the situation to suit the leader.

Situational Favourableness Factors
Leader-Member Relations

Degree of confidence, trust and respect followers have for the leader.

Task Structure

Degree to which job assignments are procedurised (high) vs ambiguous (low).

Position Power

Degree of influence a leader has — hiring, firing, discipline, promotions.

The Least Preferred Co-worker (LPC) Questionnaire identifies whether a leader is task-oriented (scores LPC low) or relationship-oriented (scores LPC high). Task-oriented leaders perform best in very favourable or very unfavourable situations; relationship-oriented leaders excel in moderate situations.

3.2 Cognitive Resource Theory

High Stress Context
Experience > Intelligence

Under stress, less intelligent but more experienced leaders outperform highly intelligent but inexperienced ones.

Low Stress Context
Intelligence > Experience

Under low stress, intellectual ability predicts leadership effectiveness better than experience.

3.3 Hersey & Blanchard — Situational Leadership

Leadership Style vs Follower Readiness
S2 — Selling/Coaching High Task + High Relationship. For followers with low ability but high willingness.
S3 — Participating Low Task + High Relationship. For followers with ability but low confidence or willingness.
S1 — Telling/Directing High Task + Low Relationship. For followers unable and unwilling.
S4 — Delegating Low Task + Low Relationship. For followers who are both able and willing.
← LOW Follower ReadinessHIGH Follower Readiness →

3.4 Leader-Member Exchange (LMX) Theory

In-Group
"Favourites"

Selected based on competence and compatibility. Receive higher quality exchanges, mentoring, and opportunities. Result: Higher performance, less turnover, greater job satisfaction.

Out-Group
"The Rest"

Receive standard transactional interactions. Lower-quality relationship with the leader. Result: Lower performance ratings and higher turnover.

Ethical Concern

LMX theory describes what naturally happens, but the in-group/out-group dynamic can create perceived favouritism, reducing morale among out-group members. Good leaders should work to expand the "in-group" inclusively.

Case Study

Army Leadership — Fiedler in Action

Military environments provide clear evidence for Fiedler's model. In combat operations (highly unfavourable situation: unclear outcomes, low position power relative to chaos), task-oriented leaders — who focus on structure and clear directives — consistently outperform relationship-oriented leaders. Studies of combat unit performance show that high task orientation correlates with better mission outcomes in ambiguous, high-threat environments.

Conversely, in peacetime training scenarios (moderately favourable), relationship-oriented leadership correlates better with troop morale and long-term skill development — exactly as Fiedler's model predicts.

Key Insight

Situational context fundamentally changes what "good leadership" looks like. A leader effective in one scenario may fail in another — reinforcing the need for situational matching over a universal "best style."

Practice Questions

Q1. What are the three key situational factors in Fiedler's model?
+

(1) Leader-Member Relations: Trust and respect between leader and followers. (2) Task Structure: How clearly defined and structured the work is. (3) Position Power: The formal authority the leader holds over rewards and punishments. These three factors combine to determine situational favourableness.

Q2. According to Hersey & Blanchard, which leadership style suits a new but highly motivated employee?
+

A new employee has low ability (lacks skills) but high willingness (motivated). This maps to a low follower readiness level — specifically R2 in Hersey & Blanchard's model. The appropriate leadership style is S2 — Selling/Coaching: high task direction combined with high relationship support to build both skills and confidence.

Q3. What ethical concerns arise from the LMX "in-group/out-group" dynamic?
+

Key concerns: Favouritism may be based on personal similarity rather than merit. Discrimination risks arise if in-group selection correlates with gender, ethnicity, or other protected characteristics. Demoralisation of out-group members reduces overall team performance. Leaders should actively work to develop high-quality relationships with all team members and make selection criteria transparent and merit-based.

Module 05 · Leadership

Types of Leadership Styles

Modern organisations require leaders who can deploy different styles depending on team, context, and goals.

11 Contemporary Leadership Styles

Style 01
Autocratic

Leader makes all decisions without input. Fast decisions, low morale in long-term contexts.

Style 02
Democratic

Involves team in decision-making. Higher buy-in, slower decisions.

Style 03
Laissez-Faire

Team given autonomy. Works with highly skilled, self-motivated teams; fails with inexperienced ones.

Style 04
Transformational

Inspires and motivates through vision. Creates lasting change in culture and performance.

Style 05
Transactional

Rewards performance, punishes failure. Clear expectations; limited inspiration.

Style 06
Bureaucratic

Rules, structure and hierarchy. Efficient in regulated industries; rigid in dynamic environments.

Style 07
Servant Leadership

Leader serves the needs of followers. High trust and purpose; may be exploited if boundaries unclear.

Style 08
Situational

Adapts style to context and follower readiness. Requires high self-awareness and flexibility.

Style 09
Visionary

Creates compelling future vision. Galvanises teams around long-term direction.

Style 10
Coaching

Develops individual potential through strengths, weaknesses and motivation identification.

Style 11
Charismatic

Uses charm, communication, and persuasiveness to inspire. High impact; can create dependence on leader.

Transformational vs Transactional

Key Distinction
TRANSACTIONAL
Manage existing order
⟷
TRANSFORMATIONAL
Change the order

Transactional leaders maintain the status quo through reward/punishment. Transformational leaders challenge followers to exceed their own interests for the collective good, fostering innovation and deep commitment.

Case Study

Nelson Mandela — Transformational + Servant Leadership

Nelson Mandela embodies the convergence of transformational and servant leadership. His transformational dimension: he articulated a compelling vision of a unified, democratic South Africa that transcended personal and factional interests, inspiring millions globally. His servant dimension: after gaining power, he consistently prioritised reconciliation over revenge — exemplified by retaining the Springbok rugby team that symbolised apartheid-era inequality, to build national unity.

The result: South Africa transitioned peacefully from apartheid to democracy — one of the 20th century's most improbable political achievements — driven largely by the example of a single leader's style.

Key Insight

The most impactful leaders often blend styles. Mandela combined transformational vision with servant values — demonstrating that leadership style is not a fixed category but a dynamic, context-sensitive practice.

Practice Questions

Q1. When is autocratic leadership appropriate and when is it harmful?
+

Appropriate: Crisis or emergency situations requiring immediate decisions; when team members lack expertise; in highly regulated environments with strict compliance needs. Harmful: Creative knowledge work requiring intrinsic motivation; long-term team development; diverse teams that need psychological safety to contribute ideas. Sustained autocratic leadership typically leads to disengagement, high turnover, and suppressed innovation.

Q2. How does transformational leadership differ from charismatic leadership?
+

Both involve inspiration, but transformational leadership focuses on elevating followers beyond self-interest toward collective goals and instils lasting behavioural change. Charismatic leadership relies on the leader's personal magnetism and may not outlast the leader — when the charismatic leader leaves, the motivation often dissipates. Transformational leaders build systems and cultures; charismatic leaders build personal followings.

Q3. What are the risks of a purely laissez-faire leadership approach?
+

Risks include: Ambiguity — without direction, teams may pursue conflicting goals. Accountability gaps — no clear structure for performance management. Inequality — confident team members dominate; quieter members become marginalised. Stagnation — without challenge or vision, innovation declines. Laissez-faire works only with highly expert, motivated, and self-directed individuals who require minimal coordination.

Managing Change

Change is inevitable. Planned change is intentional. Understanding what drives organisational change and how to manage it effectively is a core leadership competency.

Forces Driving Change

ForceExamples
Nature of WorkforceCultural diversity, ageing population, skills gaps in new entrants
TechnologyAI, cloud computing, mobile technology, genetic research
Economic ShocksFinancial crises, interest rate volatility, market crashes
CompetitionGlobal competitors, mergers, growth of e-commerce
Social TrendsChanging consumer values, social media, demographic shifts
World PoliticsTrade wars, geopolitical instability, regulatory changes

Types of Change

Personal
Individual Change

New environment, relationships, habits — the human dimension of change.

Organisational
Structural Change

New management, restructuring, technology adoption.

Type 1
Developmental

Improvement of existing skills, processes, or structures.

Type 2
Transitional

Moving from old state to a new defined state. Managed transition.

Type 3
Transformational

Radical, fundamental shifts in strategy, culture, or identity.

Societal
Cultural Shift

Digital transformation, generational value changes, social movements.

ADKAR Change Model

About ADKAR

Created by Jeffrey Hiatt, ADKAR is a people-centric model. Unlike sequential methods, each letter represents a goal to be reached — not a step in a fixed order.

ADKAR Model
A
Awareness — of the need to change
D
Desire — to participate in and support the change
K
Knowledge — on how to change
A
Ability — to implement required skills and behaviours
R
Reinforcement — to sustain the change

Kübler-Ross Change Curve

Originally developed as the five stages of grief, this model applies powerfully to organisational change — describing the emotional journey individuals experience:

Denial
Anger
Bargaining
Depression
Acceptance

Effective change managers help teams move through these stages by addressing emotions directly — not suppressing them — while maintaining momentum toward the new state.

Case Study

Nokia's Failure to Change — A Cautionary Tale

Nokia once held over 40% of the global mobile phone market. By 2013, it sold its handset division to Microsoft for a fraction of its former value. The forces of change (smartphone technology, touch interfaces, app ecosystems) were visible — but Nokia failed to respond.

Internal analysis revealed: leaders were in the "denial" phase of the change curve; siloed culture prevented cross-functional innovation; and management's emphasis on existing strengths (hardware) blinded them to the transformational shift to software. ADKAR's "Desire" was missing at the leadership level — executives did not want to cannibalise their successful feature-phone business.

Key Insight

Knowing change is needed (Awareness) is insufficient. Leaders must create Desire — an active will to change — throughout the organisation. Nokia had awareness but lacked desire, leading to catastrophic inaction.

Practice Questions

Q1. What are the five stages of the ADKAR model? Explain each briefly.
+

A — Awareness: Individuals understand why change is needed. D — Desire: They are motivated to participate and support. K — Knowledge: They know how to change (skills, processes). A — Ability: They can actually implement the change behaviourally. R — Reinforcement: Systems and rewards sustain the new behaviour long-term.

Q2. Distinguish between developmental, transitional, and transformational change.
+

Developmental change improves what already exists (e.g., training employees to use software more efficiently). Transitional change replaces the old with something new (e.g., migrating from on-premise to cloud systems). Transformational change fundamentally reinvents the organisation — strategy, culture, identity may all shift (e.g., a bank becoming a fintech company).

Q3. How does the Kübler-Ross curve apply to an employee whose role is being restructured?
+

The employee may initially deny the restructuring will really happen. Then feel anger at the perceived unfairness. They may bargain (try to negotiate keeping their old role). Experience depression as the reality sinks in. Finally reach acceptance and engage with the new role. Managers should recognise this process, provide clear communication at each stage, and not force premature acceptance — rushing employees past natural emotional stages creates resentment.

Resistance to Change

Resistance is not irrational — it is a natural human and organisational response. Understanding its sources is essential to overcoming it.

Forms of Resistance

Overt & Immediate
Visible Resistance

Voicing complaints, engaging in job actions (strikes, slowdowns), publicly opposing decisions.

Implicit & Deferred
Hidden Resistance

Loss of loyalty, motivation reduction, increased errors, growing absenteeism. Harder to detect, more damaging over time.

Individual Sources of Resistance

  • Habit: Change disrupts comfortable routines; humans naturally prefer familiarity
  • Security: Change threatens the predictability people rely on
  • Economic factors: Fear of income reduction, job loss, or reduced benefits
  • Fear of the unknown: Uncertainty about outcomes triggers anxiety
  • Selective information processing: People hear only what confirms their fears

Organisational Sources of Resistance

Source 1
Structural Inertia

Selection, training, formalisation — built-in mechanisms produce stability and act as counterbalances when change is attempted.

Source 2
Limited Focus of Change

Organisations are interdependent systems. Change in one area (e.g., technology) without corresponding changes elsewhere gets nullified.

Source 3
Group Inertia

Even if an individual accepts change, group norms (e.g., union rules) may override personal willingness.

Source 4
Threat to Expertise

Specialists resist changes that make their expertise obsolete. E.g., IT departments resisting decentralised computing in the 1980s.

Source 5
Threat to Power

Redistribution of decision-making authority threatens existing power hierarchies.

Source 6
Threat to Resources

Groups controlling sizable resources fear budget cuts or reduced staff — change threatens the current allocation.

Tactics for Overcoming Resistance

TacticWhen to UseDrawback
Education & CommunicationResistance from misinformationTime-consuming
ParticipationWhen initiators lack all informationCan be slow; risk of poor change design
Facilitation & SupportFear and anxiety driving resistanceExpensive; not always effective
NegotiationPowerful resistance group with clear interestsCan be expensive; invites others to negotiate
Manipulation & CooptationWhen other tactics are too costlyCan backfire if people feel manipulated
CoercionSpeed is essential; change agents have powerRisky; creates resentment and long-term damage

The Politics of Change

Political Reality

Change in organisations is never purely rational. Power dynamics, vested interests, and political manoeuvring always shape how change unfolds. Long-time power holders tend to implement only incremental change that doesn't threaten their position.

  • Impetus for radical change typically comes from outside change agents (consultants, new leaders)
  • Internal change agents are most threatened by loss of status
  • Power struggles determine the speed and quality of change implementation
Case Study

Kodak — Structural Inertia and Expertise Threat

Kodak engineers actually invented the digital camera in 1975 — but the technology was shelved. Why? Because digital photography threatened Kodak's core revenue: film and chemical processing. This is a textbook example of threat to established resource allocations and structural inertia.

The teams controlling Kodak's enormously profitable film division actively resisted digital initiatives. Group inertia — a culture built around film chemistry — reinforced individual resistance. By the time Kodak embraced digital, competitors had already established dominant positions. Kodak filed for bankruptcy in 2012.

Key Insight

Organisational resistance sources can kill even innovative organisations. When structural inertia and resource-protection instincts override strategic necessity, the result can be catastrophic — even when the organisation already possesses the answer.

Practice Questions

Q1. What is the difference between overt and implicit resistance to change?
+

Overt resistance is visible and immediate — strikes, public complaints, open refusal to comply. Implicit resistance is hidden and deferred — reduced motivation, quiet disengagement, increased errors, growing absenteeism. Implicit resistance is more dangerous because it is harder to detect and address, allowing damage to accumulate silently.

Q2. Why might structural inertia be the most powerful form of organisational resistance?
+

Structural inertia operates invisibly — it is embedded in selection processes, training programmes, job descriptions, and reward systems. Unlike individual resistance (which can be addressed person-by-person), structural inertia requires changing the entire organisational architecture. Even when individual employees accept change, the structure itself pulls behaviour back toward the old state.

Q3. When is coercion an appropriate tactic for overcoming resistance, and what are its risks?
+

Coercion is appropriate when: there is a genuine crisis requiring immediate action; no time exists for participation or education; and the change agent has sufficient power to enforce compliance. Risks: Deep resentment, reduced trust in leadership, compliance without commitment (people comply publicly but sabotage privately), high turnover, and long-term cultural damage. Coercion should be a last resort.

Conflict Management

Conflict is not inherently harmful. Understanding its types, causes, and management techniques determines whether conflict destroys or strengthens a group.

Defining Conflict

Conflict is a process that begins when one party perceives that another party has negatively affected, or is about to negatively affect, something the first party cares about. It encompasses:

  • Incompatibility of goals between parties
  • Differences in interpretation of facts
  • Disagreements based on behavioural expectations

Evolution of Conflict Thought

Traditional View
Conflict is Bad

All conflict is harmful. Caused by poor communication, lack of openness, or failure to respond to employee needs. Must be avoided.

Human Relations View
Conflict is Natural

Conflict is a natural and inevitable outcome in any group. Should be accepted, not eliminated.

Interactionist View
Some Conflict is Necessary

Conflict is not only a positive force — it is absolutely necessary for a group to perform effectively. Leaders should stimulate managed conflict.

Functional vs Dysfunctional Conflict

DYSFUNCTIONAL CONFLICT

Common Causes of Conflict

  • Miscommunication and misunderstandings
  • Differences in opinions, values, or personalities
  • Power struggles and competition for resources
  • Unclear roles and expectations in workplaces or teams
Impact of Unresolved Conflict

Increased stress and anxiety → reduced productivity → damaged relationships → potential team dissolution. Unmanaged dysfunctional conflict is one of the leading causes of employee turnover.

Conflict Resolution Techniques (Thomas-Kilmann)

Problem Solving

Face-to-face meeting to identify and resolve the conflict through open discussion.

Superordinate Goals

Creating a shared goal that overrides individual conflicts — both parties need each other to succeed.

Expansion of Resources

When conflict is caused by resource scarcity — resolve it by expanding the resource pool.

Avoidance

Withdrawal or suppression. Appropriate when the issue is trivial or cooling-off time is needed.

Smoothing

Minimise differences and emphasise common interests. Temporary but useful to maintain relationships.

Compromise

Each party gives up something. No clear winner or loser. Good when goals are moderately important.

Authoritative Command

Management uses formal authority to resolve conflict. Fast but may not address root causes.

Altering Human Variable

Behavioural change techniques — training, counselling — to change attitudes causing conflict.

Altering Structural Variables

Redesign jobs, transfer employees, create liaison roles, or use an ombudsperson.

Conflict Stimulation Techniques

When conflict is too low (group complacency, groupthink), managers may deliberately stimulate it:

Communication

Sending ambiguous or threatening messages to stimulate debate.

Bringing in Outsiders

Adding new members with different backgrounds and values.

Restructuring

Reorganising work groups to break established patterns.

Devil's Advocate

Appointing a person to intentionally challenge group consensus.

Case Study

Amazon's "Disagree and Commit" Culture

Amazon explicitly institutionalises functional conflict through its leadership principles. The principle "Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit" demands that leaders respectfully challenge decisions they disagree with — even when uncomfortable. Jeff Bezos famously required "six-pager" narrative memos before meetings, ensuring all data and logic were exposed to rigorous challenge before decisions were made.

Amazon's culture deliberately avoids "harmony at all costs" — recognising that surface-level agreement often masks groupthink, which kills innovation. Once a decision is made after open debate, all parties commit fully — separating the conflict phase from the execution phase.

Key Insight

Institutionalised functional conflict — structured disagreement with clear resolution processes — can drive better decisions than suppressing conflict in the name of "team harmony." The key is directing conflict toward ideas, not people.

Practice Questions

Q1. Differentiate between functional and dysfunctional conflict with examples.
+

Functional conflict: A product team debates whether to prioritise speed or security in a new app. The debate surfaces important trade-offs, leads to a better-informed decision, and improves the final product. Dysfunctional conflict: Two team members have a personal rivalry that causes them to withhold information from each other, causing project delays and team breakdown. The key distinction: functional conflict is about ideas and goals; dysfunctional conflict is about personalities and power.

Q2. When should a manager choose compromise over problem-solving as a conflict resolution technique?
+

Compromise is preferred when: time is short; the goals of both parties are moderately important (not critical); both parties have equal power; other methods have failed; and a temporary solution is needed to maintain the relationship while a fuller solution is developed. Problem-solving is preferred when there is sufficient time to explore root causes, when the issue is complex, and when a lasting, integrated solution is needed.

Q3. Explain the interactionist view of conflict and why it is considered the most useful perspective for managers.
+

The interactionist view holds that some conflict is not just inevitable but necessary for effective group performance. Too little conflict leads to complacency, groupthink, and lack of innovation. Too much conflict is destructive. The manager's role is to maintain an optimal level of conflict — stimulating it when the group is too harmonious, resolving it when it becomes dysfunctional. This is the most useful perspective because it treats conflict as a management tool, not merely a problem to eliminate.

Perception: Fundamentals

Behaviour is based on perceived reality, not objective reality. Understanding how perception works is foundational to understanding all human behaviour in organisations.

What is Perception?

Core Principle

"People's behaviour is based on their perception of what reality is, not on reality itself. The world as it is perceived is the world that is behaviourally important."

Perception is the process by which individuals organise, select, and interpret sensory stimuli to produce a meaningful picture of their environment. Two people can observe the same event and interpret it in fundamentally different ways.

Factors Influencing Perception

The Perception Model
In the Perceiver
Internal Factors

In the Target

In the Situation
Context Factors

Time · Work setting · Social setting · Environmental conditions

ALL THREE → PERCEPTION

Figure-Ground and Visual Perception

The classic figure-ground illusions demonstrate a core perceptual principle: our brain actively organises sensory input, and the same input can be organised in multiple ways. There is no single "correct" perception:

The Vase/Faces Illusion

Depending on what you treat as "figure" vs "ground," you see either a vase or two faces — the image itself doesn't change.

GOOD/EVIL Letters

The word GOOD contains the word EVIL in the white spaces — representing that good cannot exist without the absence of it.

TEACH/LEARN Reflection

The word TEACH reflects as LEARN — illustrating that teaching and learning are mirror processes.

Organisational Implication

Just as the same image can be "seen" differently, the same managerial action (e.g., close supervision) can be perceived by one employee as helpful support and by another as suffocating micromanagement.

Why Perception Matters in Organisations

  • Performance appraisals are perceptual judgements, not objective measurements
  • Customers' buying behaviour is driven by their perception of value, not actual value
  • Employee motivation depends on perceived fairness (equity theory)
  • Conflict often stems from perceptual differences rather than objective disagreements
  • Leadership effectiveness is partly a function of how followers perceive the leader
Case Study

Apple vs Samsung — Same Feature, Different Perception

When Apple launched Face ID in 2017, it removed the fingerprint sensor. The tech media largely perceived this as "Apple removing a useful feature." When Samsung later struggled with in-screen fingerprint sensors, the same perception framework worked in reverse — Samsung users perceived their own phones as innovative and Apple's as lagging.

The objective reality: both companies were managing the same biometric authentication challenge. The perceptual reality: identical technical constraints were interpreted through the lens of brand loyalty, expectations, and framing — producing completely opposite emotional responses from identical users of each brand.

Key Insight

In markets, perception IS reality for the customer. Companies that understand and manage perceptual factors (branding, framing, expectations) can maintain competitive advantage even when objective product differences are minimal.

Practice Questions

Q1. Define perception. Why is it important in organisational behaviour?
+

Perception is the process by which individuals organise, select, and interpret sensory information to give meaning to their environment. It is crucial in OB because: (1) behaviour is driven by perceived, not objective, reality; (2) managerial decisions — appraisals, promotions, conflict resolution — are all perception-mediated; (3) understanding perception allows leaders to communicate more effectively by accounting for how their actions will be interpreted, not just intended.

Q2. List the three sets of factors that influence perception and give one example of each.
+

Perceiver factors: A manager who has had bad experiences with tardiness (past experience) immediately perceives a late employee as unmotivated. Target factors: A candidate wearing bright red at an interview is noticed more than others (novelty, visual prominence). Situational factors: The same work mistake perceived differently in a high-pressure deadline situation (work setting) vs. a normal day.

Q3. How do figure-ground perception principles apply in workplace communication?
+

The figure-ground principle shows that perception involves selective attention — the "figure" receives focus while the "ground" recedes. In communication: what the sender considers the main point (figure) may be perceived as background by the receiver, who focuses on different aspects (e.g., tone, context, or implications). This is why clear, explicit communication is critical — what seems obvious to the communicator may not be what the audience perceives as the focal point.

Attribution Theory

When we observe behaviour, we instinctively ask "why?" Attribution theory explains how we answer that question — and where we go wrong.

Core Concept

Proposed by Kelley (1972), attribution theory suggests that when we observe someone's behaviour, we try to determine whether it was caused internally (by the person's character, choices, ability) or externally (by the situation, luck, or environment).

Internal Attribution
Personal Cause

Behaviour is under the personal control of the individual. "She succeeded because she's talented." "He failed because he's lazy."

External Attribution
Situational Cause

Behaviour is caused by outside forces. "She succeeded because the market was favourable." "He failed because the system was unfair."

Three Key Factors in Attribution

Kelley's Attribution Factors
Factor 1
Distinctiveness

Does the individual behave the same way in other situations? High distinctiveness (unusual behaviour) → external cause. Low distinctiveness (same everywhere) → internal cause.

Factor 2
Consensus

Do others in the same situation respond the same way? High consensus (everyone does it) → external cause. Low consensus (only this person) → internal cause.

Factor 3
Consistency

Does the person behave this way regularly? High consistency (always does this) → internal cause. Low consistency (rare occurrence) → external cause.

Restaurant Complaint Example
FactorLevelObservationAttribution
DistinctivenessHighCustomer rarely complains elsewhere→ External
ConsensusHighMany customers complained today→ External
ConsistencyHighThis restaurant gets frequent complaints→ External
All three high → External AttributionPoor restaurant quality

Attribution Errors and Biases

Bias 1
Fundamental Attribution Error

We overestimate internal factors and underestimate external factors when judging others. "He's late because he's irresponsible" (ignoring traffic). We blame people's character for situational problems.

Bias 2
Self-Serving Bias

We attribute our successes to ourselves (internal) and our failures to the situation (external). "I passed because I'm intelligent. I failed because the exam was unfair."

Managerial Implication

Fundamental attribution error leads managers to blame employees personally for performance problems that are actually systemic. This results in the wrong interventions: individual retraining when process redesign is needed.

Case Study

Performance Reviews & Fundamental Attribution Error

A global consulting firm studied 5,000 performance reviews and found a systematic pattern: when high-performers missed targets, managers attributed it to external factors ("the market was tough"). When the same managers reviewed low-performers missing identical targets, they attributed it to internal factors ("lacks the drive to succeed").

This is a classic demonstration of both the fundamental attribution error (over-attributing low-performer failures to personality) and a halo/in-group bias effect (protecting high-performers through external attributions). The result: systematically unfair performance reviews that reinforced pre-existing hierarchies.

Key Insight

Attribution biases are not neutral — they have real consequences for careers, promotions, and organisational fairness. Structured performance review processes (blind evaluations, calibration sessions) are designed to counteract these systematic errors.

Practice Questions

Q1. What are the three factors Kelley used to determine internal vs external attribution?
+

Distinctiveness: Is the behaviour unique to this situation (external) or consistent across contexts (internal)? Consensus: Do others behave the same way in this situation (external) or only this individual (internal)? Consistency: Is this behaviour regular for this person (internal) or an anomaly (external)?

Q2. What is the fundamental attribution error? Give an organisational example.
+

The fundamental attribution error is the tendency to overestimate internal (character) factors and underestimate external (situational) factors when judging others' behaviour. Example: An employee consistently submits work late. The manager concludes "she's just not disciplined" (internal attribution), ignoring that her workload is double the team average, her tools are outdated, and she's covering two roles due to understaffing (external factors).

Q3. How does self-serving bias affect a leader's learning and growth?
+

Self-serving bias prevents accurate self-assessment. Leaders who attribute all success to their own brilliance and all failure to external circumstances miss crucial feedback loops. They fail to learn from mistakes, repeat poor decisions, and may develop overconfidence. Leaders who counteract this bias — asking "what could I have done differently?" after failures — demonstrate superior long-term performance and adaptability.

Perceptual Biases & Decision-Making

Systematic errors in how we perceive and interpret information distort every decision we make. Knowing these biases is the first step to mitigating them.

Common Perceptual Shortcuts

Shortcut 1
Selective Perception

We interpret what we see based on our background, experience, and attitudes — filtering out information that doesn't fit our existing views.

Shortcut 2
Halo Effect

Drawing a general impression of a person based on a single characteristic. "She's well-dressed → she must be competent."

Shortcut 3
Contrast Effects

Evaluation of a person is affected by comparison with others recently encountered. A "good" candidate seems exceptional after three poor interviews.

Shortcut 4
Projection

Attributing our own characteristics to other people. A dishonest manager assumes their team is also dishonest.

Shortcut 5
Stereotyping

Judging someone based on perception of the group to which they belong — ignoring individual variation.

Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

Pygmalion Effect vs Golem Effect
Pygmalion Effect (+)
High Expectations → High Performance

When leaders hold high expectations of followers and communicate this belief, followers rise to meet those expectations. The belief creates the reality.

Golem Effect (−)
Low Expectations → Low Performance

When leaders hold low expectations, they unconsciously communicate this through tone, body language, and reduced support — creating the poor performance they expected.

Management Application

Leaders who genuinely believe in their team's potential — not just performatively — produce measurably better team outcomes. Self-fulfilling prophecy is not mystical; it operates through real behavioural mechanisms: allocation of stretch assignments, coaching investment, feedback frequency.

Impact of Perceptual Biases on Decisions

Case Study

Google's "Project Oxygen" — Golem Effect in Management

Google's Project Oxygen (2008) studied thousands of managers and their teams. A striking finding: managers who scored lowest on a new internal metric for "believing in their team's potential" had significantly lower team performance, higher attrition, and worse psychological safety scores — even when controlling for team composition and task difficulty.

Conversely, managers who genuinely held high expectations (not just expressed them) systematically produced higher-performing teams. The mechanism: high-expectation managers gave harder problems, more developmental feedback, and more autonomy — creating the conditions in which teams exceeded expectations.

Key Insight

The Pygmalion/Golem effect is not soft psychology — it translates directly into hard performance metrics. Google's data showed that a manager's genuine belief in their team predicts team performance as strongly as team members' actual skill level.

Practice Questions

Q1. What is the halo effect and how does it affect hiring decisions?
+

The halo effect is the tendency to let one positive (or negative) trait colour our overall assessment of a person. In hiring: if a candidate is exceptionally articulate in an interview, interviewers may rate them higher on unrelated dimensions (technical skill, attention to detail) simply because the positive first impression radiates outward. Structured interviews with standardised scoring criteria are designed to counteract halo effects.

Q2. Distinguish between Pygmalion and Golem effects with workplace examples.
+

Pygmalion: A new team lead tells a junior developer "I think you're ready to lead the next feature release." The developer receives more autonomy and guidance, rises to the challenge, and delivers successfully — fulfilling the leader's positive expectation. Golem: A manager consistently assigns the same employee "routine" tasks because she doubts their strategic capability. Denied stretch opportunities, the employee's skills stagnate — confirming the manager's low initial assessment.

Q3. How can organisations reduce the impact of perceptual biases in performance management?
+

Key interventions: (1) Calibration sessions: Managers discuss and align ratings in groups, surfacing individual biases. (2) Structured rating scales: Behavioural anchors reduce subjectivity. (3) 360-degree feedback: Multiple rater perspectives counteract single-rater bias. (4) Blind evaluations: Remove identifying information (names, photos) from initial assessments. (5) Bias training: Make managers aware of their specific bias patterns using data from past reviews.

Decision-Making: Types & Tools

From intuition to structured frameworks — effective decision-making requires knowing which approach fits the situation, and which tools sharpen your thinking.

Individual Decision-Making

Individual decision-making involves a single person identifying and choosing a course of action from several alternatives based on their own judgment, values, knowledge, and perception. The process includes:

Recognise Problem
→
Gather Info
→
Evaluate Options
→
Make Choice
→
Own Outcome

Types of Decision-Making

Type 1
Rational

Step-by-step logical approach: define problem → identify criteria → weigh criteria → generate alternatives → evaluate → select best. Used for high-stakes, structured decisions with available data.

Example: A company comparing cost, quality and ROI before selecting a supplier.

Type 2
Intuitive

Based on instincts and pattern recognition from past experience. Fast, tacit, and often surprisingly accurate for experts in their domain.

Example: Experienced manager quickly senses which candidate is suitable in an interview.

Type 3
Emotional

Influenced by feelings, biases, and mood states. Common, often unconscious, and can produce poor outcomes when unrecognised.

Example: Buying an expensive product due to excitement or brand attachment.

Perception and Decision-Making Link

Core Principle

Decisions are made by interpreting and evaluating information. The perceiver's frame determines: which data sources are consulted, which data are considered relevant, and how alternatives are framed. Two rational decision-makers with identical data but different perceptual frameworks will make different decisions.

Decision-Making Tools

Tool 1
SWOT Analysis

Maps Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats. Useful for strategic decisions under uncertainty.

Tool 2
Cost-Benefit Analysis

Quantifies expected benefits against costs. Brings financial rigour to trade-off decisions.

Tool 3
Pros & Cons List

Simple enumeration of advantages and disadvantages. Best for low-stakes, rapid decisions.

Tool 4
Pareto Analysis

80/20 rule: focus on the 20% of causes generating 80% of problems. Prioritisation tool.

Tool 5
Decision Matrix

Evaluates options against weighted criteria systematically. Reduces intuitive bias in multi-factor decisions.

Six Thinking Hats (Edward de Bono)

A structured framework for parallel thinking — each "hat" represents one perspective, ensuring a decision is examined from all angles before commitment:

🎩
White Hat

Facts and data only. What do we know? What information do we need? No opinions here.

🟡
Yellow Hat

Optimism and benefits. What is the best possible outcome? What value does this create?

⚫
Black Hat

Caution and risk. What can go wrong? What are the weaknesses and dangers?

❤️
Red Hat

Emotions and intuition. What does your gut say? What are the feelings behind this?

🟢
Green Hat

Creativity and alternatives. What other approaches exist? What new ideas are possible?

🔵
Blue Hat

Process and control. How should we think about this? Manages the thinking process itself.

Why Six Thinking Hats Works

By separating thinking types, groups avoid the common failure of mixing fact, emotion, and criticism simultaneously — which produces confused, emotionally charged debates. Each hat is "worn" sequentially by the whole group, ensuring all perspectives are explored without conflict.

Case Study

NASA and the Challenger Disaster — Failure of Rational Decision-Making

The 1986 Space Shuttle Challenger disaster is one of history's most analysed decision-making failures. Despite engineers' rational data showing O-ring failure risk in cold temperatures, the launch decision was ultimately driven by: confirmation bias (management focused on data confirming readiness), availability bias (no previous O-ring catastrophe made one feel unlikely), and social pressure from NASA's schedule commitments.

Had the Six Thinking Hats framework been applied formally — forcing explicit Black Hat thinking (what can go wrong?) and White Hat thinking (what does the temperature data actually show?) — the structural issues preventing safety concerns from reaching decision-makers might have been overcome.

Key Insight

Even technically sophisticated organisations fail at decision-making when perceptual biases, social pressures, and poor process override rational analysis. Structured decision frameworks are not bureaucratic overhead — they save lives.

Practice Questions

Q1. Compare rational, intuitive, and emotional decision-making. When is each appropriate?
+

Rational: Best for complex, high-stakes, reversible decisions with available data (e.g., supplier selection, investment decisions). Intuitive: Best for time-pressured, expert-domain decisions where pattern recognition is reliable (e.g., emergency medical decisions, chess moves). Emotional: Rarely optimal alone, but emotional signals (discomfort with an ethical grey area, excitement about creative work) can be valuable input when consciously integrated — not when they unconsciously override logic.

Q2. Explain how the Six Thinking Hats technique improves group decision-making.
+

Six Thinking Hats improves group decisions by: (1) Ensuring all dimensions (facts, risks, benefits, creativity, emotions, process) are explicitly considered — teams can't skip the Black Hat (risk analysis) because it's uncomfortable. (2) Reducing ego-driven conflict — everyone is wearing the same hat simultaneously, so criticism is directed at ideas not people. (3) Expanding the solution space — Green Hat thinking pushes beyond the first solution to explore alternatives. (4) Managing meeting dynamics — the Blue Hat structures the process, preventing one voice from dominating.

Q3. How does perception link to the quality of decisions made in organisations?
+

Perception is the gateway through which all information enters decision-making. Poor perception → poor decisions: (1) Selective perception causes decision-makers to miss critical signals. (2) Halo effects distort evaluation of options. (3) Stereotyping limits consideration of diverse alternatives. (4) Anchoring bias causes over-reliance on initial information. Improving perceptual awareness — through diverse teams, structured processes, and deliberate data collection — is therefore one of the highest-leverage ways to improve decision quality.
