📘 Tutorial 8 - DC Transient Analysis

Q8.1 RC Circuit Analysis - Find R, C, Time Constant
Medium
In the circuit shown in Fig. 1, find: (a) the values of R and C, (b) calculate the time constant, (c) determine the time required for the voltage to decay to half its initial value at t = 0.
Given: R = 7 kΩ, C = 0.7143 × 10⁻⁶ F, τ = 5 × 10⁻³ sec, t = 3.466 × 10⁻³ sec
📋 Given Data
  • Resistance R = 7 kΩ = 7000 Ω
  • Capacitance C = 0.7143 μF = 0.7143 × 10⁻⁶ F
  • Time constant τ = 5 ms = 5 × 10⁻³ s
🔬 Step 1: Verify Time Constant
$$\\tau = R \times C = 7000 \times 0.7143 \times 10^{-6}$$ $$\\tau = 5.0001 \times 10^{-3} \text{ s} \approx 5 \text{ ms}$$

Verification: The calculated time constant matches the given value ✓

📐 Step 2: Half-Life Time Calculation

For RC discharge: v(t) = V₀e^(-t/τ)

At half voltage: v(t)/V₀ = 0.5 = e^(-t/τ)

$$\ln(0.5) = -\frac{t}{\\tau}$$ $$t = -\\tau \times \ln(0.5) = \\tau \times \ln(2)$$ $$t = 5 \times 10^{-3} \times 0.693 = 3.465 \times 10^{-3} \text{ s}$$
🧮 Step 3: Alternative Method (Differentiation)

Rate of voltage decay at t=0:

$$\frac{dv}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0} = -\frac{V_0}{\\tau}$$

If decay continued at this rate: v = V₀ - (V₀/τ)t

For v = V₀/2: t = τ/2 = 2.5 ms (approximation)

Actual: t = τln(2) = 3.466 ms (exact)

✅ Final Answers:
(a) R = 7 kΩ, C = 0.7143 μF
(b) Time Constant τ = RC = 5 ms
(c) Half-life time t₁/₂ = τln(2) = 3.466 ms
💡 Exam Trick: Remember t₁/₂ = 0.693τ for RC circuits. For RL circuits, current half-life is also t₁/₂ = 0.693τ where τ = L/R.
Q8.2 Time Constant from Circuit Diagram
Easy
Determine the time constant for the circuit in Fig. 2.
Answer: τ = 3.22 × 10⁻⁶ sec
🔬 Analysis

For any RC circuit, the time constant is:

$$\\tau = R_{eq} \times C_{eq}$$

Where Rₑq is the Thevenin resistance seen by the capacitor.

📐 Calculation Steps

1. Identify all resistors in the circuit

2. Find equivalent resistance (series/parallel combination)

3. Multiply by capacitance value

$$\\tau = R_{th} \cdot C = 3.22 \times 10^{-6} \text{ s}$$ $$\\tau = 3.22 \mu\text{s}$$
✅ Answer: τ = 3.22 μs
Q8.3 Switching Circuit - Voltage Decay
Medium
The switch in Fig. 3 has been in position A for a long time. At t = 0, it moves to B. Find v(t) for t > 0.
Answer: v(t) = 40e^(-50t) V, t > 0
📋 Initial Conditions (t = 0⁻)

Switch at A for long time → capacitor fully charged → acts as open circuit

$$v(0^-) = v(0^+) = V_0 = 40 \text{ V}$$

Key Concept: Capacitor voltage cannot change instantaneously

🔬 Circuit for t > 0

Switch moves to B → capacitor discharges through resistor

Thevenin resistance seen by capacitor: Rₜₕ

$$\\tau = R_{th} \cdot C = \frac{1}{50} = 0.02 \text{ s}$$
📐 General Solution

For RC discharge with initial voltage V₀:

$$v(t) = V_0 e^{-t/\\tau} = 40e^{-50t} \text{ V}$$

Where the exponent coefficient: 1/τ = 50 s⁻¹

🧮 Verification by Differentiation
$$\frac{dv}{dt} = -50 \times 40e^{-50t} = -2000e^{-50t}$$

At t = 0: dv/dt = -2000 V/s

Initial rate: -V₀/τ = -40/0.02 = -2000 V/s ✓

✅ Answer: v(t) = 40e^(-50t) V for t > 0
Q8.4 RL Circuit - Current Decay
Medium
For the circuit in Fig. 4, find i₀(t) for t > 0.
Answer: i₀(t) = 1.412e^(-3t) A, t > 0
📋 Initial Conditions

Before switching (t = 0⁻):

$$i_L(0^-) = i_L(0^+) = I_0$$

Inductor current cannot change instantaneously

🔬 RL Circuit Analysis

For t > 0, the circuit becomes a source-free RL circuit

$$\\tau = \frac{L}{R_{eq}}$$

From the given solution: 1/τ = 3 → τ = 1/3 s

📐 Current Expression
$$i_0(t) = I_0 e^{-t/\\tau} = 1.412e^{-3t} \text{ A}$$

Initial current I₀ = 1.412 A (from steady-state analysis)

✅ Answer: i₀(t) = 1.412e^(-3t) A for t > 0
Q8.5 Complex RL Circuit with Initial Conditions
Hard
Consider the circuit of Fig. 5. Find v₀(t) if i(0) = 2 A and v(t) = 0.
Answer: v₀(t) = -2e^(-16t) V, t > 0
📋 Given Information
  • Initial inductor current: i(0) = 2 A
  • Source voltage: v(t) = 0 (shorted)
  • Circuit contains inductor with stored energy
🔬 Energy Dissipation

With v(t) = 0, the circuit becomes source-free. The inductor discharges its stored energy:

$$W = \frac{1}{2}Li^2 = \frac{1}{2}L(2)^2 = 2L \text{ J}$$
📐 Time Constant Determination

From the exponent: 1/τ = 16 → τ = 1/16 s

$$\\tau = \frac{L}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{16} \text{ s}$$
🧮 Voltage Calculation

Output voltage across a component:

$$v_0(t) = L\frac{di}{dt} = L \times (-16) \times (-2e^{-16t})$$ $$v_0(t) = -2e^{-16t} \text{ V}$$

Negative sign indicates opposite polarity

✅ Answer: v₀(t) = -2e^(-16t) V for t > 0

📗 Tutorial 9 - Diode Applications

Q9.1 Diode Current with Forward Bias
Easy
The current flowing in a PN-junction diode at room temperature is 200 nA when a large reverse bias is applied. Calculate the current through the diode when 0.1V forward bias is applied.
Answer: 9.165 μA
📋 Given Data
  • Reverse saturation current: Iₛ = 200 nA = 200 × 10⁻⁹ A
  • Forward voltage: V = 0.1 V
  • Temperature: Room temperature (300 K)
🔬 Diode Equation
$$I = I_s\left(e^{V/\\eta V_T} - 1\right)$$

Where V_T = kT/q = 26 mV at room temperature (300K)

For silicon: η = 1 (ideal factor)

📐 Calculation
$$\frac{V}{V_T} = \frac{0.1}{0.026} = 3.846$$ $$e^{3.846} = 46.83$$ $$I = 200 \times 10^{-9} \times (46.83 - 1)$$ $$I = 200 \times 10^{-9} \times 45.83 = 9.165 \times 10^{-6} \text{ A}$$
🧮 Alternative: Using Natural Log
$$\ln\left(\frac{I}{I_s} + 1\right) = \frac{V}{V_T}$$ $$I = I_s(e^{V/V_T} - 1)$$
✅ Answer: I = 9.165 μA
💡 Key Point: At room temperature, V_T ≈ 26 mV. Remember: for every 60 mV increase in forward voltage, diode current increases by 10× (decade rule).
Q9.2 Silicon Diode Circuit Design
Medium
In the given circuit, a silicon diode with knee voltage 0.7V is used. Dynamic resistance is zero in forward bias.
(a) If V_DD = 5V, find R for I = 5mA
(b) At V_DD = 6V, find power in R and diode D for I = 5mA
Answer: (a) 860Ω (b) 26.5mW, 3.5mW
📋 Circuit Analysis
V_DD ──[ R ]──┬──[Diode]──┐ │ │ GND GND

Applying KVL: V_DD = I·R + V_D

🔬 Part (a): Find R
$$V_{DD} = I \cdot R + V_T$$ $$5 = 5 \times 10^{-3} \cdot R + 0.7$$ $$R = \frac{5 - 0.7}{5 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{4.3}{0.005} = 860 \Omega$$
📐 Part (b): Power Calculations at V_DD = 6V

For I = 5mA with V_DD = 6V:

$$V_R = V_{DD} - V_T = 6 - 0.7 = 5.3 \text{ V}$$ $$P_R = I^2 R = I \cdot V_R = 5 \times 10^{-3} \times 5.3 = 26.5 \text{ mW}$$ $$P_D = I \cdot V_T = 5 \times 10^{-3} \times 0.7 = 3.5 \text{ mW}$$
🧮 Verification
$$P_{total} = P_R + P_D = 26.5 + 3.5 = 30 \text{ mW}$$ $$P_{source} = V_{DD} \cdot I = 6 \times 5 = 30 \text{ mW}$$

✓ Power conservation verified

✅ Answers:
(a) R = 860 Ω
(b) P_R = 26.5 mW, P_D = 3.5 mW

Q9.4 Diode Circuit with Forward Resistance
Hard
The diodes in the circuit are identical with V_T = 0.6V and R_F = 10Ω. Determine V_D, I_D and V_o.
Answer: V_D = 1.833V, I_D = 31.667mA, V_o = 3.167V
📋 Given Data
  • Threshold voltage: V_T = 0.6 V per diode
  • Forward resistance: R_F = 10 Ω per diode
  • Multiple diodes in series/parallel combination
🔬 Circuit Model

Each diode replaced by: V_T (battery) + R_F (resistor) in series

$$\text{Diode Model: } V_T = 0.6V, \quad r_f = 10\Omega$$
📐 KVL Application

Applying KVL to the complete circuit:

$$V_{source} = I_D \cdot R_{total} + n \cdot V_T + I_D \cdot n \cdot R_F$$

Where n = number of diodes in series path

🧮 Solving for Current
$$I_D = \frac{V_{source} - nV_T}{R_{load} + nR_F}$$ $$I_D = 31.667 \text{ mA}$$
📊 Voltage Calculations
$$V_D = nV_T + I_D \cdot nR_F = 1.833 \text{ V}$$ $$V_o = I_D \cdot R_{load} = 3.167 \text{ V}$$
✅ Answers:
V_D = 1.833 V, I_D = 31.667 mA, V_o = 3.167 V

Q9.9 Multi-Diode Circuit with Cut-in Voltage
Hard
All diodes are identical with cut-in voltage 0.6V. Find V_o and I for (a) V_i = 0V and (b) V_i = 2V.
Answer: (a) V_o = 0.6V, I = 4.7mA (b) V_o = 1.8V, I = 4.1mA
📋 Circuit Description

Multiple diodes in series with resistor network

Each diode: V_T = 0.6V when conducting

🔬 Part (a): V_i = 0V

Diodes need forward bias to conduct:

$$V_o = n \times V_T = 1 \times 0.6 = 0.6 \text{ V}$$

(n = number of conducting diodes)

$$I = \frac{V_{supply} - V_o}{R} = 4.7 \text{ mA}$$
📐 Part (b): V_i = 2V
$$V_o = V_i - nV_T = 2 - 0.2 = 1.8 \text{ V}$$ $$I = \frac{V_{supply} - V_o}{R_{total}} = 4.1 \text{ mA}$$
✅ Answers:
(a) V_o = 0.6 V, I = 4.7 mA
(b) V_o = 1.8 V, I = 4.1 mA

Q10.1 Half-Wave Rectifier Design
Easy
The turn ratio of transformer in half-wave rectifier is N₁:N₂ = 12:1. Primary is connected to 220V, 50Hz mains. Diode resistance is zero in forward bias. Calculate DC voltage across load and diode PIV rating.
Answer: V_dc = 8.25V, PIV = 25.93V
📋 Given Data
  • Turns ratio: N₁/N₂ = 12/1
  • Primary voltage: V₁(rms) = 220 V
  • Frequency: f = 50 Hz
  • Diode: Ideal (r_f = 0, V_T = 0)
🔬 Step 1: Secondary Voltage
$$\frac{V_2}{V_1} = \frac{N_2}{N_1}$$ $$V_2(rms) = \frac{220}{12} = 18.33 \text{ V}$$ $$V_m = \sqrt{2} \times V_2(rms) = \sqrt{2} \times 18.33 = 25.93 \text{ V}$$
📐 Step 2: DC Output Voltage

For half-wave rectifier:

$$V_{dc} = \frac{V_m}{\\pi} = \frac{25.93}{3.1416} = 8.25 \text{ V}$$

Formula derivation:

$$V_{dc} = \frac{1}{2\\pi}\int_0^{\\pi} V_m \sin(\\omega t) d(\\omega t) = \frac{V_m}{\\pi}$$
🧮 Step 3: PIV Rating

Peak Inverse Voltage is the maximum reverse voltage across diode:

$$PIV = V_m = 25.93 \text{ V}$$

During negative half-cycle, entire secondary voltage appears across diode

Q10.2 Practical Half-Wave Rectifier Analysis
Medium
A half-wave rectifier uses silicon diode with r_f = 10Ω and V_T = 0.7V. Load R_L = 500Ω. Transformer N₁:N₂ = 10:1, primary 220V, 50Hz. Calculate:
(a) DC voltage across load (b) Rectifier efficiency (c) PIV rating
Answer: [Calculated values]
📋 Given Data
  • r_f = 10 Ω, V_T = 0.7 V (Si diode)
  • R_L = 500 Ω
  • N₁/N₂ = 10, V₁ = 220 V
🔬 Secondary Voltage
$$V_2 = \frac{220}{10} = 22 \text{ V (rms)}$$ $$V_m = 22\sqrt{2} = 31.11 \text{ V}$$
📐 Peak Current
$$I_m = \frac{V_m - V_T}{R_L + r_f} = \frac{31.11 - 0.7}{500 + 10} = \frac{30.41}{510} = 59.63 \text{ mA}$$
🧮 Part (a): DC Voltage
$$V_{dc} = \frac{I_m R_L}{\\pi} = \frac{59.63 \times 10^{-3} \times 500}{\\pi} = 9.49 \text{ V}$$

Or: V_dc = (V_m - V_T)/π × R_L/(R_L + r_f) ≈ 9.49 V

📊 Part (b): Efficiency
$$\\eta = \frac{40.6}{1 + r_f/R_L} = \frac{40.6}{1 + 10/500} = \frac{40.6}{1.02} = 39.8\%$$

Maximum theoretical efficiency for HWR = 40.6%

📈 Part (c): PIV
$$PIV = V_m = 31.11 \text{ V}$$
✅ Answers:
(a) V_dc ≈ 9.49 V
(b) η ≈ 39.8%
(c) PIV = 31.11 V

Q10.4 Bridge Rectifier Complete Analysis
Hard
Bridge rectifier with V_T = 0V, r_f = 10Ω. Secondary resistance R_S = 10Ω. Load = 970Ω. Secondary voltage V_S = 20sin(ωt) V. Determine:
(a) PIV (b) DC current (c) DC power (d) Efficiency
Answer: [Complete analysis required]
📋 Given Data
  • V_S = 20sin(ωt) → V_m = 20 V
  • r_f = 10 Ω per diode (4 diodes)
  • R_S = 10 Ω (secondary winding)
  • R_L = 970 Ω
  • V_T = 0 (ideal threshold)
🔬 Part (a): PIV

For bridge rectifier:

$$PIV = V_m = 20 \text{ V}$$

Each diode sees V_m reverse voltage when not conducting

📐 Part (b): DC Current

Total resistance in conducting path:

$$R_{total} = R_S + 2r_f + R_L = 10 + 2(10) + 970 = 1000 \Omega$$ $$I_m = \frac{V_m}{R_{total}} = \frac{20}{1000} = 20 \text{ mA}$$ $$I_{dc} = \frac{2I_m}{\\pi} = \frac{2 \times 20}{\\pi} = 12.73 \text{ mA}$$
🧮 Part (c): DC Power
$$P_{dc} = I_{dc}^2 R_L = (12.73 \times 10^{-3})^2 \times 970 = 157.3 \text{ mW}$$
📊 Part (d): Efficiency
$$P_{ac} = I_{rms}^2 (R_L + R_S + 2r_f)$$ $$I_{rms} = \frac{I_m}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{20}{\sqrt{2}} = 14.14 \text{ mA}$$ $$\\eta = \frac{P_{dc}}{P_{ac}} = \frac{157.3}{200} = 78.6\%$$

Or using formula: η = 81.2%/(1 + losses) ≈ 78.6%

✅ Answers:
(a) PIV = 20 V
(b) I_dc = 12.73 mA
(c) P_dc = 157.3 mW
(d) η = 78.6%

Theory Notes

Concise exam-oriented revision material

⚡ RC & RL Transient Circuits

▼

🔑 Key Concepts

  • Time Constant: τ = RC (for RC) or τ = L/R (for RL)
  • Capacitor voltage cannot change instantaneously
  • Inductor current cannot change instantaneously
  • Steady state: capacitor → open circuit, inductor → short circuit
  • Transient: circuit behavior during switching

📐 Important Formulas

RC Charging: v_C(t) = V(1 - e^(-t/τ))

RC Discharging: v_C(t) = V₀e^(-t/τ)

RL Growth: i_L(t) = (V/R)(1 - e^(-t/τ))

RL Decay: i_L(t) = I₀e^(-t/τ)

Time Constant: τ = RC or L/R

📊 Time Constant Values

tChargingDischarging
τ63.2%36.8%
2τ86.5%13.5%
3τ95.0%5.0%
5τ99.3%0.7%

🎯 Exam Tips

Initial Conditions: v_C(0⁺) = v_C(0⁻), i_L(0⁺) = i_L(0⁻)
Final Values: v_C(∞) = source voltage, i_L(∞) = V/R
General Form: x(t) = x(∞) + [x(0⁺) - x(∞)]e^(-t/τ)
⚠️ Most Important: The universal formula for any first-order circuit:
$$x(t) = x(\infty) + [x(0^+) - x(\infty)]e^{-t/\\tau}$$
Where x can be voltage or current

📝 Viva Questions

Why can't capacitor voltage change instantaneously? ▼
Because v = q/C, and charge q cannot change instantaneously as it requires infinite current (i = dq/dt). Energy stored in capacitor (½CV²) cannot change abruptly.
What is the significance of 5τ? ▼
After 5 time constants, the transient is considered complete (99.3% change). This is the practical settling time for engineering calculations.
Why prefer RC over RL circuits? ▼
1. Inductors are bulky, heavy, and costly
2. Inductors have non-ideal behavior (resistance, core losses)
3. Magnetic fields can induce unwanted voltages
4. Capacitors are easier to fabricate in ICs

🔌 Diodes & Rectifiers

▼

🔑 Diode Characteristics

  • Forward Bias: P positive, N negative → conducts
  • Reverse Bias: P negative, N positive → blocks
  • Threshold Voltage: Si = 0.7V, Ge = 0.3V
  • Reverse Saturation Current: Si ≈ nA, Ge ≈ μA
  • Breakdown: Zener (< 4V) or Avalanche (> 6V)

📐 Diode Equation

$$I = I_s\left(e^{V/\\eta V_T} - 1\right)$$

V_T = kT/q ≈ 26 mV at 300K
η = 1 (Ge), 2 (Si) for recombination

DC Resistance: R_F = V_D/I_D

AC Resistance: r_f = ΔV_d/ΔI_d = V_T/I_D

Approximation: r_f ≈ 25mV/I_D(mA) Ω

📝 Viva Questions

Why is Si preferred over Ge for diodes? ▼
1. Higher temperature stability (Si: 200°C, Ge: 75°C)
2. Lower reverse saturation current
3. Higher breakdown voltage
4. SiO₂ provides excellent passivation
What is the significance of V_T = 26 mV? ▼
V_T = kT/q is the thermal voltage. At room temperature (300K), it equals approximately 26 mV. It represents the voltage equivalent of temperature and is crucial in diode and transistor equations.

🔬 PN Junction Physics

▼

🔑 Formation

  • P-type: Holes are majority carriers
  • N-type: Electrons are majority carriers
  • Junction formed by diffusion (not mechanical contact)
  • Depletion region: Immobile ions, no free carriers

📐 Depletion Region

Barrier Potential:

V₀ = 0.7 V (Si), 0.3 V (Ge)

Electric Field: E = V₀/W

Width: W ∝ √(V₀/N_A·N_D)

⚡ Biasing Effects

ParameterForwardReverse
Depletion WidthDecreasesIncreases
Barrier HeightDecreasesIncreases
CurrentLarge (mA)Small (μA/nA)
ResistanceLowHigh

🔧 Breakdown Mechanisms

  • Zener Breakdown: V_Z < 4V, high field breaks covalent bonds
  • Avalanche Breakdown: V_Z > 6V, carrier multiplication by impact ionization
  • Temperature Coefficient: Negative for Zener, positive for Avalanche

📝 Viva Questions

Why is depletion region called so? ▼
It is depleted of free charge carriers. The mobile electrons and holes have diffused across the junction, leaving behind immobile positive donor ions on N-side and negative acceptor ions on P-side.
What is barrier potential? ▼
The potential difference across the depletion region that opposes further diffusion of majority carriers. It is created by the uncovered immobile ions and represents the energy barrier for carrier crossing.

🔧 BJT Transistors

▼

🔑 Construction

  • Types: NPN and PNP
  • Doping: E > C > B (Emitter heaviest)
  • Width: C > E > B (Base thinnest)
  • Base: Lightly doped and thin (crucial for transistor action)

📐 Current Relations

$$I_E = I_C + I_B$$ $$I_C = \beta I_B + (\beta + 1)I_{CBO}$$ $$\alpha = \frac{I_C}{I_E}, \quad \beta = \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}$$

Typical values: α ≈ 0.98-0.998, β ≈ 50-300

🎯 Biasing Requirements

  • Active Region: EB forward, CB reverse (amplification)
  • Saturation: Both junctions forward (switch ON)
  • Cutoff: Both junctions reverse (switch OFF)
  • Inverse Active: EB reverse, CB forward (rarely used)

📝 Viva Questions

Why is base made thin and lightly doped? ▼
Thin base ensures most injected carriers reach the collector (high transport factor). Light doping reduces recombination in the base, increasing current gain β. If base were thick/heavily doped, most carriers would recombine, making I_C ≈ 0.
What is the significance of α and β? ▼
α (common-base current gain) = I_C/I_E ≈ 0.98-0.998. β (common-emitter current gain) = I_C/I_B = α/(1-α). β represents the amplification factor - small base current controls large collector current.

🕸️ Network Theory

▼

🔑 Fundamental Laws

KCL: ΣI_in = ΣI_out at any node

KVL: ΣV_rise = ΣV_drop in any loop

Ohm's Law: V = IR

📐 Network Theorems

  • Thevenin: V_th = open-circuit voltage, R_th = dead network resistance
  • Norton: I_N = short-circuit current, R_N = R_th
  • Superposition: One source at a time, others deactivated
  • Maximum Power: R_L = R_th for max power transfer

⚡ Source Transformation

Voltage → Current: I_s = V_s/R_s (parallel R_s)

Current → Voltage: V_s = I_s·R_s (series R_s)

📊 Electrical Science Fundamentals

▼

🔑 Basic Concepts

  • Current: Rate of charge flow (I = dQ/dt)
  • Voltage: Potential difference (V = W/Q)
  • Power: P = VI = I²R = V²/R
  • Energy: W = Pt = VIt

📐 AC Fundamentals

v(t) = V_m sin(ωt + φ)

V_rms = V_m/√2, I_rms = I_m/√2

ω = 2πf, T = 1/f

For 50Hz: T = 20ms, ω = 314 rad/s

⚡ Power in AC

Real Power: P = VI cos φ (Watts)

Reactive Power: Q = VI sin φ (VAR)

Apparent Power: S = VI (VA)

Power Factor: cos φ = R/|Z| = P/S

🔧 Resonance

Series: f₀ = 1/(2π√(LC))

Bandwidth: BW = f₀/Q

Q-factor: Q = ω₀L/R = 1/(ω₀CR)

At resonance: Z = R (minimum), I = V/R (maximum)

Exam Tips & Tricks

Strategies to maximize your score

⏱️ Time Management

Allocate time based on marks. For a 3-hour exam with 100 marks, spend ~1.8 minutes per mark. Attempt easier questions first to build confidence.

📝 Step-by-Step Solutions

Always write formulas first, then substitute values. Even if calculation is wrong, formula marks are awarded. Show all steps clearly.

🔢 Unit Consistency

Convert all units to standard form (Ω, F, H, V, A) before calculation. Watch for kΩ, mA, μF conversions.

📊 Graph Sketching

Label axes, mark important points (peak, zero crossings), indicate time constants. Use ruler for straight lines. Show at least 2-3 cycles for periodic waveforms.

⚡ Diode State Check

Always verify diode assumption. If assumed ON, check current flows from anode to cathode. If assumed OFF, check V_AK < V_T.

🔄 Rectifier Identification

Count diodes: 1=HWR, 2=CT-FWR, 4=Bridge. Check transformer: CT present → CT-FWR possible. PIV doubles for CT-FWR compared to Bridge.

📐 Thevenin Shortcut

For transient problems, find R_th seen by energy element (L or C). Deactivate sources: voltage source → short, current source → open.

🎯 Initial/Final Conditions

Remember: v_C(0⁺) = v_C(0⁻), i_L(0⁺) = i_L(0⁻). At t=∞: C→open, L→short. These are crucial for transient analysis.

📈 Approximation Valid

For V >> V_T: I ≈ I_s e^(V/ηV_T). For V < 0: I ≈ -I_s. Use appropriate approximation to simplify calculations.

✅ Final Answer Format

Box final answers. Include units. For efficiency, express as percentage. For ripple factor, keep 3 decimal places. Write conclusions for design problems.

Frequently Asked Viva Questions

Prepare for oral examinations

⚡ RC/RL Circuits - Viva Questions

▼
What is the physical significance of time constant? ▼
Time constant τ represents the time required for the response to reach 63.2% of its final value (for growth) or decay to 36.8% of initial value (for decay). It indicates how fast the circuit responds to changes. After 5τ, the transient is practically complete.
Why does capacitor block DC but pass AC? ▼
For DC (f=0), capacitive reactance X_C = 1/(2πfC) = ∞, so capacitor acts as open circuit. For AC, X_C is finite and decreases with frequency, allowing current to pass. Physically, capacitor charges to DC voltage and then no more current flows.
What happens if R=0 in an RL circuit? ▼
Time constant τ = L/R becomes infinite. The current would never reach steady state. In practice, there's always some resistance (wire resistance), so τ is finite but very large, making the circuit respond very slowly.

🔌 Diodes - Viva Questions

▼
What is the difference between knee voltage and cut-in voltage? ▼
They are essentially the same - the minimum forward voltage required for significant current flow. Knee voltage refers to the "knee" point on the V-I curve where current starts increasing rapidly. Cut-in voltage is the practical threshold (0.7V Si, 0.3V Ge).
Why does reverse saturation current double for every 10°C rise? ▼
Because I_s depends on n_i² (intrinsic carrier concentration), and n_i² ∝ T³ e^(-E_g/kT). The exponential temperature dependence dominates, causing approximately doubling every 10°C. This is why Si is preferred - its lower I_s makes it more temperature stable.
Can a diode be used as a switch? Explain. ▼
Yes. Forward biased diode acts as closed switch (low resistance, ~0.7V drop). Reverse biased diode acts as open switch (very high resistance, only leakage current). This switching property makes diodes useful in digital circuits and rectifiers.

⚡ Rectifiers - Viva Questions

▼
Why is the ripple factor important? ▼
Ripple factor measures the effectiveness of rectification. Lower ripple means purer DC output. High ripple can damage sensitive electronic components. Filter circuits are added to reduce ripple. FWR (r=0.482) is better than HWR (r=1.21).
Why can't we get 100% rectification efficiency? ▼
Due to: (1) Diode forward voltage drop (V_T), (2) Diode forward resistance (r_f), (3) Transformer winding resistance, (4) Ripple in output. These losses prevent achieving ideal efficiency. Maximum theoretical: HWR=40.6%, FWR=81.2%.
What is the advantage of bridge rectifier over center-tap? ▼
1. No center-tap transformer needed (cheaper transformer)
2. PIV = V_m (half of CT-FWR's 2V_m), so lower voltage diodes can be used
3. Better transformer utilization factor
4. Only disadvantage: 4 diodes instead of 2 (higher voltage drop)
